Caesar Cipher

The Caesar Cipher is one of the simplest and oldest encryption techniques. It works by shifting each letter of the plaintext by a fixed number of places down or up the alphabet. For example, with a shift of 3, A becomes D, B becomes E, and so on. This method is named after Julius Caesar, who used it to communicate securely.

The Caesar Cipher is a substitution cipher where each letter of the plaintext is replaced by a letter some fixed number of positions down the alphabet. Though simple and easy to break, it was historically used for military communication.

Vigenère Cipher

The Vigenère Cipher is a method of encrypting alphabetic text through a series of Caesar ciphers based on the letters of a keyword. The Vigenère Cipher is considered a stronger version of the Caesar Cipher because it uses multiple shifts, making it harder to crack.

The cipher uses a table called the Vigenère square or Vigenère table, where each row corresponds to a Caesar shift of the alphabet. The key is repeated over the length of the message, and for each letter in the plaintext, a corresponding letter from the key is used to find the shift for encryption.
